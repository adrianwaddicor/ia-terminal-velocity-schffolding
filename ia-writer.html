Section 1: Research Design

6 marks

1.1.1 State your full research question

Write your research question naming the independent variable (parachute surface area in cm²) and the dependent variable (terminal velocity in m/s).

Hints

Your RQ must name both variables explicitly. “How does area affect speed?” is too vague — include units in your framing.

Specify whether this is an experimental or secondary data investigation — the marker needs to know from the first paragraph.

1.1.2 Background theory

Explain the concept of terminal velocity: the point at which drag force equals gravitational force, producing zero net force and constant velocity.

State the drag force equation: Fdrag = ½ρCAv², where ρ is air density, C is the drag coefficient, A is the cross-sectional area, and v is velocity.

Explain why increasing surface area increases drag at a given speed, which lowers the terminal velocity reached by the system.

Hints

Define every symbol in your equations. A marker should not have to look anything up.

Warning

Background theory must be directly relevant — do not pad with general kinematics. Everything here should connect to why surface area affects terminal velocity.

1.2.1 Variables

Independent variable: surface area of the parachute canopy (cm²). State the values you will test and why you chose this range.

Dependent variable: terminal velocity (m/s). Explain how you will determine when terminal velocity is reached and how you will measure it.

Control variables: mass of the falling object, shape of canopy (circular), drop height, string length, air conditions (indoors, no wind). For each one, state the method of control.

Hints

For each control variable, explain HOW you controlled it, not just that you did. “The mass was kept constant by using the same 50 g mass in every trial” scores higher than “mass was controlled.”

1.2.2 Measurement method

Describe how you measure terminal velocity — e.g. using video analysis software (Tracker) to measure displacement between frames, or light gates at two known positions near the bottom of the drop.

State the precision of your measuring instruments: ruler (±0.5 mm), timer resolution, camera frame rate.

Explain how many trials you will run per surface area value and justify this number with reference to reducing random error.

Hints

If using video analysis: state the frame rate of your camera and how you calibrated the scale in the footage.

Warning

Avoid writing “I repeated the experiment 3 times for accuracy.” Instead, say: “Three trials were conducted per surface area value so that an average could be calculated and anomalous results identified.”

1.2.3 Safety and practical considerations

Note any safety considerations: dropping from height — ensure the drop zone is clear of people and obstacles.

Note any environmental controls: experiment conducted indoors to minimise air current variation.

1.3.1 Step-by-step procedure

List the specific materials: circular plastic sheet cut to 5 different areas (e.g. 50, 100, 150, 200, 250 cm²), 50 g mass, string of fixed length (30 cm), measuring tape, camera or light gates.

Write a numbered procedure specific enough that another student could replicate it without asking you any questions.

Include: how you cut and measured canopy area, how you attached the mass, the drop height, how you recorded the fall, and how you extracted velocity from your data.

Hints

The test of a good method section: give it to a classmate who wasn’t in the lab. If they have questions, add that detail.

Warning

Do not write a generic method — every step should be specific to this investigation. Avoid phrases like “appropriate equipment was used.”

Section 2: Data Analysis

6 marks

2.1.1 Raw data table

Create a table with surface area (cm²) in the first column and terminal velocity measurements (m/s) for each trial in subsequent columns. Include a column for the mean terminal velocity.

Include uncertainty values for your measurements in the column headers.

Hints

All velocity values in the same column must have the same number of decimal places. If your instrument reads to 2 decimal places, record every value to 2 decimal places — including 1.20, not 1.2.

Table title goes above the table; graph title goes below the graph (as a caption). Include units in headers, not in the cells: write “Terminal velocity / m/s” not “Terminal velocity (m/s measured in metres per second).”

Warning

Never mix significant figures across the same column. “1.234, 1.2, 1.23” in one column is a precision error and will cost marks.

2.2.1 Calculate mean and uncertainty

Calculate the mean terminal velocity for each surface area value.

Calculate the uncertainty in the mean using half the range: uncertainty = (max − min) / 2.

Show at least one sample calculation in full, clearly labelling inputs and outputs.

Hints

Sample calculations should show the formula, substitution with values and units, and the final answer. One worked example is enough — do not repeat it for every row.

Propagate uncertainties through any further calculations. If you calculate v², the percentage uncertainty doubles.

2.2.2 Graph description and gradient

Plot mean terminal velocity (y-axis, m/s) against surface area (x-axis, cm²) with error bars on each point reflecting your uncertainty.

Add a best-fit line or curve. If the relationship is non-linear (theory predicts v ∝ A−0.5), consider plotting v² vs 1/A to linearise.

Determine the gradient of your best-fit line with its uncertainty using max and min gradient lines through the error bars.

Hints

Both axes need: a label, the quantity, and units (e.g. “Terminal velocity / m s&supmin;¹”). The origin does not need to be included unless physically meaningful.

If using software (Logger Pro, Desmos, Google Sheets): state which software you used and how the fit was applied. Do not just paste a graph with no explanation.

Warning

A best-fit line is NOT a dot-to-dot. It should be smooth and balanced through the data. Draw it with a ruler or use software — freehand curves are not acceptable.

Section 3: Conclusion

6 marks

3.1.1 State and justify your conclusion

Directly answer the research question: as the surface area of the parachute increases, the terminal velocity decreases. State the direction and nature of the relationship (e.g. inverse, non-linear).

Refer specifically to your data: “As surface area increased from 50 cm² to 250 cm², mean terminal velocity decreased from X ± δX m/s to Y ± δY m/s.”

State whether your graph supports a linear or non-linear relationship and what this implies physically.

Hints

Your conclusion must be consistent with your processed data including uncertainties. If error bars overlap between two data points, you cannot claim a significant difference between those values.

Warning

Do not introduce new data or claims here. Every statement must follow from your analysis section.

3.2.1 Comparison to accepted scientific context

Return to the drag force equation from your introduction: does your data support the predicted relationship v ∝ A−0.5? Explain why or why not using your gradient.

Compare your measured terminal velocities to any published or theoretical values where available, calculating a percentage difference.

Explain any deviation from the theoretical prediction with reference to your uncertainties — is the discrepancy within your experimental uncertainty?

Hints

Cite all sources in sufficient detail: author, title, year, and URL or publisher. A URL alone is not a complete citation.

Warning

Superficial comparison (“my results agree with theory”) scores 1–2. Justify the comparison: “my experimental gradient of X ± δX is consistent with the theoretical value of Y, since Y lies within the uncertainty range.”

Section 4: Evaluation

6 marks

4.1.1 Identify specific weaknesses and their impact

For each weakness, describe: (1) what the weakness is, (2) how it specifically affected your data or conclusion, and (3) a realistic, specific improvement.

Example — Canopy shape deformation: the parachute may not have maintained a constant circular profile during the fall, meaning the effective surface area differed from the measured area, potentially overestimating terminal velocity at larger sizes.

Example — Insufficient drop height: if the drop height was insufficient for all parachute sizes to reach terminal velocity, the measured velocity is not truly terminal, and the relationship between area and vt is understated.

Example — Air currents: even indoors, slight air movement could systematically affect results for larger parachutes more than smaller ones, introducing a confounding variable.

Hints

Rank your weaknesses by impact on the conclusion. The most significant weakness should come first and receive the most discussion.

Warning

“Human error” and “measurement error” are generic and score 1–2. Name the specific source: e.g. “parallax error when reading the ruler scale” or “variation in release angle between trials.”

4.2.1 Propose specific, realistic improvements

For each weakness, propose an improvement that directly addresses it. Generic improvements (“use better equipment”) do not score.

Improvement for canopy deformation: attach a lightweight rigid ring around the perimeter of each canopy to maintain a consistent circular profile during the fall.

Improvement for insufficient drop height: conduct a preliminary test calculating the theoretical terminal velocity for each canopy size and ensure drop height is at least 3× the distance needed to reach 99% of vt.

Improvement for air currents: use a purpose-built vertical drop tube (e.g. transparent acrylic cylinder) to eliminate lateral air movement entirely.

Hints

Each improvement must be matched to a specific weakness. If you list 4 weaknesses and 2 improvements, you will lose marks — every weakness needs a corresponding improvement.

Warning

Extensions (more trials, wider range) are not improvements to weaknesses — they are extensions to scope. Keep these separate or omit them unless asked.

General Writing Hints — apply across all sections

  • Write in the past tense for what you did; present tense for what the theory says.
  • Use passive voice for methodology: “The mass was released from a height of 2.0 m” rather than “I dropped the mass.”
  • Every numerical claim needs a unit. Every unit needs a numerical claim.
  • When you use a word like “significant,” “large,” or “small,” follow it immediately with the actual value.
  • Do not use the word “prove” — experiments support, suggest, or are consistent with a conclusion.
  • Avoid personal opinion language: “I think,” “I believe,” “in my opinion” have no place in a scientific report.
