Section 1: Research Design

6 marks

1.1.1 State your full research question

Your research question needs to name your independent variable (like parachute surface area in cm²) and your dependent variable (like terminal velocity in m/s).

Briefly describe the setup you used: what kind of falling mass, how you changed the canopy area, and where you dropped it to keep things controlled.

Quick Tips

Make sure both variables are explicitly named with units. Don't just say "How does area affect speed?" That's too vague! Say exactly what you measured.

Tell the reader right away whether you collected this data yourself in the lab or used secondary data from a simulation or database.

1.1.2 Background theory

Explain terminal velocity in plain English: what happens when the upward drag force balances out the downward pull of gravity?

Lay out the main equation: drag force equals half the air density times the drag coefficient, area, and velocity squared.

Connect the dots: why does a bigger surface area mean more drag, and how does that change the final speed?

Quick Tips

Define every single letter or symbol in your equations. Your examiner shouldn't have to guess what your variables mean.

Watch Out

Keep your focus tight. Don't talk about general motion or history unless it directly explains why your surface area changes your terminal velocity.

1.2.1 Variables

Independent variable: What did you change? State your canopy areas (in cm²) and explain why you picked that specific range.

Dependent variable: What did you measure? Explain exactly how you tracked the terminal velocity and how you knew it actually stopped accelerating.

Control variables: What did you keep the same? Think about the mass, shape, drop height, and room conditions. Explain exactly how you kept them constant.

Quick Tips

Be specific about your controls. Don't just say "the mass was controlled." Say: "I used the exact same 50 g mass for every single drop." That's what gets the marks!

1.2.2 Measurement method

How did you capture the speed? Did you use video analysis software like Tracker, or did you set up light gates near the bottom of the drop zone?

What was the precision of your tools? Write down the limits of your ruler (like ±0.5 mm), your timer, or your camera's frame rate.

How many times did you repeat each drop? Explain why doing multiple trials helps cut down on random errors.

Quick Tips

If you used video tracking, mention your camera's frame rate and exactly how you calibrated the scale in the video frames.

Watch Out

Never write "I did it 3 times for accuracy." Instead, write: "I did three trials for each area so I could calculate an average and spot any weird, anomalous data."

1.2.3 Safety and practical considerations

Any safety risks? Since you're dropping things from a height, how did you make sure the landing zone was clear of people and fragile gear?

Any environmental issues? Mention if you worked indoors to avoid stray drafts or air currents from messing with the falls.

1.3.1 Step-by-step procedure

List all your kit: the exact sizes of your canopies, the masses, string lengths, tape measures, and camera setups.

Write out a numbered, clear recipe of your experiment. A classmate should be able to read this and run the whole thing perfectly without asking you for help.

Include details on how you cut the canopies, how you rigged the mass, the drop height, and how you processed the raw footage or times.

Quick Tips

The ultimate test: hand your method to a friend. If they have a question about how to do a step, you need to add more detail to that step!

Watch Out

Avoid generic steps like "use appropriate equipment." Say exactly what piece of kit you picked up and how you used it.

Section 2: Data Analysis

6 marks

2.1.1 Raw data table

Build a clean data table. Put your surface areas in the first column, followed by columns for your individual trial speeds, and finish with a column for the calculated average speed.

Don't forget to include your absolute uncertainties right up in the column headers.

Quick Tips

Keep your decimal places matching down every column. If your stopwatch reads to two decimal places, write 1.20, not 1.2!

Put your table title at the top, and graph captions at the bottom. Keep units in the headers (like "Velocity / m/s"), not scattered next to every single number in the cells.

Watch Out

Never mix significant figures in the same column. Mixing entries like 1.234, 1.2, and 1.23 shows sloppy decimal management and will lose you marks.

2.2.1 Calculate mean and uncertainty

Find the mean terminal velocity for every single canopy size you tested.

Calculate the uncertainty for your averages using the range method: (max value minus min value) divided by 2.

Show one sample calculation completely out in the open—step-by-step with formulas, numbers, and units.

Quick Tips

Your sample calculation needs to show the raw equation, how you swapped in your numbers, and the final answer with units. Just show one example—no need to repeat it for all five rows.

If you did extra math (like squaring the velocity), remember that percentage uncertainties change (they double if you square the value!).

2.2.2 Graph description and gradient

Plot your average terminal velocity on the y-axis against surface area on the x-axis. Make sure every single data point has a clear error bar matching your calculated uncertainty.

Draw a smooth trendline. If it looks like a curve, think about linearising your data (like plotting velocity squared against 1/Area) to turn it into a straight line.

Find the slope (gradient) of your best-fit line. Use maximum and minimum lines of best fit through your error bars to figure out the uncertainty of that slope.

Quick Tips

Label both axes clearly with quantities and units (e.g., "Terminal velocity / m s⁻¹"). Only include the point (0,0) if it makes physical sense for your experiment.

If you used Logger Pro, Desmos, or Excel, say so! Briefly explain how you applied the fit instead of just pasting a mystery chart.

Watch Out

A trendline is NOT a connect-the-dots game. It should be a balanced, clean line running through the middle of your points. Use graphing software or a physical ruler—freehand squiggles won't cut it.

Section 3: Conclusion

6 marks

3.1.1 State and justify your conclusion

Answer your research question directly: as the parachute's surface area opens up, what happens to the terminal velocity? Explain the mathematical nature of this trend (is it an inverse pattern, a curve, or straight line?).

Back it up with numbers: "When the surface area went up from 50 cm² to 250 cm², the average terminal velocity dropped from X ± δX m/s down to Y ± δY m/s."

What does the shape of your graph tell us about the real-world physics of the falling object?

Quick Tips

Your conclusion has to match your actual numbers and error bars. If your error bars overlap heavily between two setups, you can't claim there's a massive physical difference between them!

Watch Out

Don't introduce surprise facts or new data here. Keep it strictly focused on explaining what you already found in your analysis section.

3.2.1 Comparison to accepted scientific context

Look back at your drag equation from the introduction: does your experimental slope match what the physics textbooks predict? Work out a percentage difference if you can find textbook values.

If your results drifted away from the theoretical values, use your uncertainty ranges to explain it—is the difference small enough to be covered by your experimental errors?

Quick Tips

Cite your sources properly. Include the author, title, year, and a working link. Just dropping a bare URL without context looks messy and unprofessional.

Watch Out

Saying "my results perfectly match the theory" without proof will only score 1 or 2 marks. Instead, show it: "My experimental gradient of X ± δX is consistent with the textbook value of Y because Y falls right inside my error buffer."

Section 4: Evaluation

6 marks

4.1.1 Identify specific weaknesses and their impact

For every flaw or limitation, break down three things: (1) what the problem was, (2) exactly how it skewed your data numbers, and (3) a smart, practical fix for next time.

Example — Canopy flapping/crumpling: The fabric didn't stay perfectly round as it fell. This changed the active surface area, which probably caused us to overestimate the terminal velocity on the larger sizes.

Example — Not enough drop room: If the ceiling was too low, the larger parachutes might not have finished accelerating. That means our speed measurements aren't true terminal velocities.

Example — Hidden air currents: Even indoors, AC vents or moving bodies create small air currents that push lighter or larger canopies off course, introducing a sneaky variable.

Quick Tips

Sort your weaknesses by how badly they hurt your results. Talk about your biggest experimental nightmare first and give it the most detail.

Watch Out

Blaming "human error" or "ruler mistakes" is lazy and scores very low. Call out the precise issue: instead, write "parallax errors when tracking the ruler mark" or "slight tilt variations when releasing the canopy."

4.2.1 Propose specific, realistic improvements

Match an actionable fix to every single weakness you listed above. Vague statements like "get better equipment" won't earn marks.

Fix for canopy crumpling: We could construct a super-light rigid wire frame around the edge of each canopy to force it to stay perfectly circular throughout the fall.

Fix for low drop heights: Run a quick math check first to estimate the terminal velocity, then make sure you find a stairwell or balcony that gives you at least three times the space needed to hit stable speed.

Fix for air currents: Drop the system inside a long, clear plastic tube to shield the falling parachute from room drafts completely.

Quick Tips

Keep your fixes balanced. If you find four weaknesses, make sure you outline four corresponding improvements. Leaving a weakness hanging without a fix will lose you marks.

Watch Out

Suggesting extensions (like testing more sizes or tracking different shapes) is not the same as fixing an error. Keep your quality fixes separate from scope changes!

General Writing Hints — apply across all sections

  • Use the past tense when describing what you did in the lab, but shift to the present tense when explaining permanent laws of physics.
  • Stick to formal scientific styling for your final report text: write "The mass was dropped" rather than "I dropped the mass."
  • Every number needs its unit buddy. Don't write a standalone number without its unit, and don't write a unit without its number.
  • Whenever you use descriptive words like "significant," "large," or "tiny," immediately back it up with the real numerical value.
  • Never use the word "prove." In physics, our data can only support, suggest, or align with a theory—we never "prove" it 100%.
  • Keep your personal opinions out of the write-up. Phrases like "I think," "I feel," or "in my opinion" have no place in a lab report.
